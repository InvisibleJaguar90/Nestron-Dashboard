The build phase of a factory-built ADU looks different from what most homeowners expect.
↑ Feed shows ~3 lines then "See More"  ·  ↓ Full post
Factory production removes the open-ended variables of site construction: weather exposure, rotating trades, and sequential scheduling decisions. The unit is built under controlled conditions by the same team running the same production process.
By the time it ships, electrical, plumbing, cabinetry, and finishes are installed and inspected. What's in the spec sheet is what arrives at your property.
What remains on site is specific: foundation work, utility connections, crane placement. These are defined before production begins, not worked out after.
If you're looking at how a factory-built ADU actually comes together, this week's post covers it.

Review Notes
Format
Template 1: Educational text post with image. The workhorse format for Facebook — suited for process education content where the audience is evaluating a high-ticket purchase and needs to understand what the build process actually looks like before they can feel confident moving forward.
Angle
Process walk-through (see Repurposing Angle Framework in social/platform_guidelines.md). Directly mirrors Draft 06's primary thesis — factory production removes the open-ended variables that make site construction unpredictable. Framed for the homeowner's actual concern: will the build process be manageable, and will I get what was specified? The psychological mechanism is Activation Energy reduction — the post answers the "what does this actually involve?" question that stalls homeowners at the consideration stage. Hook is framed around a gap in expectation ("looks different from what most homeowners expect"), not around the product or the company.
Truncation
Facebook truncates after approximately 3 lines on mobile. The hook — "looks different from what most homeowners expect" — lands inside that window and creates an open loop. The "See More" tap resolves it: the body explains what's actually different and why it matters. Single short sentence above the fold is intentional — it stops the scroll without over-promising.
Image
Exterior shot of the L2 unit. The Morongo photos (Ext_ series) are the strongest available asset for this audience — a real unit on a real site directly addresses the legitimacy question that is active for buyers at this stage. Ext_Back_Rows.jpg is the current fallback; substitute any clean, well-lit Morongo exterior if a stronger shot is available. Crop to 1200 × 630px for Facebook's preferred feed ratio, or 1080 × 1350px (4:5) to fill more vertical screen real estate. No text overlays on the image.
Timing
Best posting windows: 8–10 AM, 12–1 PM, or 6–8 PM. Tuesday through Thursday perform strongest. Reply to comments promptly — Facebook's algorithm distributes Page content to more users similar to those who engage with it.
Red Lines
✓ Pass — No ROI claims, no permit approval guarantees, no timeline commitments, no competitor references. No cost comparisons or project cost estimates — post stays on manufacturing process and what the unit delivers, not on pricing. Anti-AI pattern check: no em dashes; no "It's not X, it's Y" constructions; no stacked short declaratives used for rhythm; "weather exposure, rotating trades, and sequential scheduling decisions" is a functional descriptive list, not a rhetorical triplet; no inflated abstractions or avoid-list vocabulary.
